CAR-T-CELL
CAR-T cell therapy is a type of immunotherapy that involves the genetic modification of a patient's own T cells, a type of white blood cell, to recognize and attack cancer cells.
T cells are an important part of the immune system and play a central role in fighting infections and cancer.

In CAR-T cell therapy, T cells are collected from a patient's blood and sent to a laboratory, where they are genetically modified to express a protein called a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR).
The CAR allows the T cells to recognize and bind to specific proteins, called antigens, that are found on the surface of cancer cells. Once the modified T cells are returned to the patient's body, they can recognize and attack cancer cells that express the target antigen.

CAR-T cell therapy has shown promise in the treatment of certain types of cancer, particularly blood cancers such as leukemia and lymphoma. It has the potential to be more effective than traditional cancer treatments, such as chemotherapy and radiation therapy, and has fewer side effects. However, CAR-T cell therapy is still considered an experimental treatment and is not widely available. It is typically used only in clinical trials or as a last resort for patients who have not responded to other treatments.
